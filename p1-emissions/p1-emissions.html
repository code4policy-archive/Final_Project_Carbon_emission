President Biden has pledged that the United States will reach net-zero emissions by 2050


But the United States is not a monolith, and its 50 states have different economies, energy mixes, politics, and, as a result, different emissions levels. To ensure that the U.S. achieves its net-zero goals, it is necessary to understand which states are the highest carbon emitters, which are the lowest carbon emitters, and the lessons that can be learned from each.


Data source: U.S. Energy Information Agency

The state with the highest carbon emissions per capita in 2018 was Wyoming, with 109.9 metric tons emitted per resident of the state. The states with the lowest emissions, meanwhile, were California and New York, at only 9 metric tons per resident. The median state emitted 16.3 metric tons per capita in 2018.


However, per capita emissions alone don't tell the whole story. It is, instead, the absolute amount of carbon emitted that will determine whether or not the world is able to stay below its target of a temperature increase of 1.5 degrees Celsius. Here, the story is quite different when we look at absolute carbon emissions by state. Wyoming is below the median in carbon emissions, whereas more populous states like California, Texas and Florida have the highest carbon emissions.

Transportion, electric power and industrial sectors are the biggest contributers to the carbon emissions whereas emissions from residental and commercial sectors are the lowest. This is true for states with the highest absolute level of carbon emissions.

Finally, the type of fossil fuel matters a great deal for how much carbon is emitted. In particular, coal emits far more carbon dioxide per unit of energy produced than diesel or natural gas, and a recent study examining the pathways across which the United States could reach its net-zero goals determined that, across all pathways, coal use had to be almost entirely eliminated by 2050.

Achieving net-zero emissions by 2050 is possible. However, doing so will take concerted action from within all 50 states.


There is some good news. The level of coal production in the United States now is lower than it was 30 years ago.

The level of coal production in 2020 was around 500 thousand short tons, compared to over 1 million thousand short tons in 1990.
